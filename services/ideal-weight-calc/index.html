About this ideal weight calculator

This ideal weight calculator answers 'how much should I weigh?' from your height and sex, showing four classic clinical formulas side by side plus a healthy weight range. There is no single correct number, so seeing the spread — and, if you add your current weight, where you sit relative to a healthy band — is more honest than one figure. It works in cm or ft/in and kg or lb.

How to use it

Select your sex and enter your height in centimetres or feet and inches. Optionally add your current weight in kg or lb. Tap Calculate and you'll see ideal body weight from the Devine, Robinson, Miller and Hamwi formulas as four cards, plus a healthy weight range from a normal BMI. If you entered a current weight, a badge tells you whether you're within, below or above that range and by how much.

The formulas, with an example

Each formula starts from a base weight at 5 ft (60 inches) and adds a fixed amount per inch above that. Devine for men is 50 kg + 2.3 kg per inch over 60. A 175 cm man is 68.9 inches, so 8.9 inches over 60: 50 + 2.3 × 8.9 ≈ 70.5 kg. Robinson, Miller and Hamwi use different constants and land near 68.7–72.0 kg. The healthy range comes from BMI 18.5–24.9 × height², here about 56.7–76.3 kg.

How to read the numbers

Read the four figures as a corridor, not a bullseye: they ignore frame size, age and how much muscle you carry, so a lean, muscular person can sit above every 'ideal' number and still be perfectly healthy. When you just want a sensible target for your height, lean on the BMI-based healthy range instead, and use your current-weight badge to see the direction of travel rather than chasing one decimal.

Devine, Robinson, Miller or Hamwi — which ideal-weight formula should I trust?

None of them is the single "true" answer. All four estimate ideal body weight from height and sex, but they were derived from different populations, so they disagree by a few kilograms — that spread is exactly why this ideal weight calculator shows them side by side. The Devine formula (1974) is the most widely used and was originally built for medication dosing; Robinson and Miller (both 1983) usually give slightly lower figures; Hamwi (1964) is the oldest quick rule of thumb. Read them as a range, not a target to the decimal.

Why show a healthy weight RANGE instead of one ideal number?

Because no single number fits every healthy body. The four formulas give point estimates, but real bodies differ in bone structure and muscle. The healthy weight range here comes from the WHO's healthy BMI band (18.5–24.9) applied to your height, so it answers "how much should I weigh" as a span rather than one figure. Sitting anywhere inside that range is generally considered a healthy weight for your height.

Why do men and women get different ideal weights at the same height?

On average, men carry more muscle and denser bone than women at the same height, so every one of these formulas sets a higher baseline for males. That is why the calculator asks for your sex before computing your ideal body weight. The healthy BMI range, by contrast, is identical for both sexes, because BMI itself does not separate by sex.

Ideal weight vs healthy weight vs BMI — what is the difference?

Ideal (body) weight is a single target figure from a formula like Devine or Hamwi. A healthy weight is a range you can sit anywhere within, taken here from a healthy BMI. BMI is the underlying number (weight ÷ height²) used to define that band. This tool shows all three at once: four ideal-weight figures, the healthy weight range, and — if you add your current weight — where you fall relative to it.

Is my height and weight sent to a server?

No. Every calculation in this ideal weight calculator runs entirely in your browser; your height, sex and weight never leave your device. Your last entry is saved only in your browser's localStorage for convenience on your next visit, and you can clear it anytime.
